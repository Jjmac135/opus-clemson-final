Leadership & Structure

How Opus Is Governed

The Opus Society is student-led and student-run. Its governance structure is designed to give students real ownership over the organization while ensuring continuity, accountability, and institutional stability year over year.

Executive Board

Leadership Roles

The Executive Board governs the Opus Society and is responsible for organizational strategy, program delivery, member selection, and institutional continuity. Each role carries real responsibility — this is not ceremonial leadership.

President

Overall organizational leadership and external representation. Serves as the primary liaison to the faculty advisor and Clemson administration. Holds final authority on strategic decisions and is responsible for the fellowship's long-term direction.

The President sets the tone for the entire organization. This role requires someone who can manage upward (faculty, administration), manage laterally (board), and manage downward (fellows) simultaneously.

Vice President

Supports the President in day-to-day operations and steps in as acting President during absences. Coordinates across the Executive Board to ensure alignment and follow-through. Often owns special projects and cross-functional initiatives.

The VP is the connective tissue of the board — the person who makes sure things don't fall through the cracks between roles.

Director of Recruitment & Mentorship

Owns the full recruitment cycle: outreach, applications, review, and selection. Manages mentor-fellow matching, tracks mentorship meeting cadence, and is the primary point of contact for alumni engagement and relations.

Recruitment determines the quality of every cohort. Mentorship determines whether fellows actually develop. This role shapes both.

Director of Programming & Logistics

Designs and executes all fellowship programming for both Year One and Year Two. Manages the deliverable calendar, event scheduling, speaker coordination, and logistics for any fellowship travel or external engagements.

Programming is the product. This role is responsible for ensuring every meeting and event is substantive, well-run, and worth fellows' time.

Director of Communication

Manages the fellowship's external brand, social media presence, website, and campus visibility. Owns internal communications to fellows and coordinates outreach to prospective applicants, campus partners, and the broader Clemson community.

How Opus is perceived on campus directly affects the quality of applicants it attracts. This role is responsible for that perception.

Faculty Support

Faculty Advisor Role

The Opus Society maintains a faculty or staff advisor affiliated with Clemson University. The advisor serves in an advisory capacity — not a managerial one. The Opus Society is student-led, and the advisor's role is to provide institutional stability and guidance, not to run the organization.

The faculty advisor is the bridge between the Opus Society and the university — ensuring the organization remains registered in good standing, providing continuity when leadership turns over annually, and offering guidance when the Executive Board faces decisions that benefit from institutional perspective.

The time commitment is intentionally light. The advisor attends one meeting per semester and is available for occasional consultation. We ask for presence and guidance, not management.

Time Commitment

One meeting per semester, plus availability for occasional consultation. Approximately 2–4 hours per academic year of active engagement.

Voting Authority

The faculty advisor does not hold voting authority on the Executive Board. Governance decisions rest with student leadership.

Charter Review

Any amendment affecting the fellowship's core mission, membership criteria, or program structure must be reviewed by the advisor prior to adoption.

Dissolution Authority

The fellowship may only be dissolved by unanimous board vote and written concurrence of the faculty advisor — a safeguard against hasty decisions.

Continuity

Elections & Terms

Executive Board members serve one-year terms. Elections are held each November, ensuring incoming leadership is seated before the spring semester and fully settled before leading fall recruitment. This structure is designed to prevent the chaos of mid-year leadership transitions.

November

Elections Held

Current fellows vote for incoming Executive Board before the fall semester closes.

December

Transition Period

Outgoing board members complete transition documents and meet with successors.

January

New Board Seated

Incoming board takes full ownership at the start of the spring semester.

August

Recruitment Begins

Board leads fall recruitment for the incoming cohort — fully settled and ready.

Founding Document

Charter Principles

The Opus Society operates under a founding charter that establishes its mission, governance structure, and core commitments. The charter is a living document — amendable by two-thirds board vote — but its core principles are designed to outlast any individual leadership team.

Amendments

Charter amendments require a two-thirds majority vote of the full Executive Board, with proposed changes circulated at least one week before the vote. Core mission, membership criteria, or program structure amendments also require faculty advisor review.

Decision Making

Routine operational decisions may be made by the President. Decisions affecting program structure, cohort selection, or charter provisions require a majority vote of the full Executive Board. No single board member may unilaterally alter the fellowship's structure or mission.

Financial Oversight

All organizational expenditures must be approved by the President. The Executive Board reviews the organization's financial standing at the start of each semester. Financial records are maintained and available to the faculty advisor upon request.

No Dues Commitment

Membership in the Opus Society carries no dues. The fellowship is committed to ensuring that financial circumstance is not a barrier to participation. Any costs associated with optional programming — such as travel — shall be subsidized to the extent funding allows.
