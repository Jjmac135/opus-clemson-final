Specialization

Choose Your Path

In Year Two, every Opus Fellow declares a career track. From that point forward, your mentorship, your deliverables, and your professional connections are all tailored to where you're actually headed. Three founding tracks are available — with more to come as the fellowship grows.

Track One

Law & Justice

The Law & Justice track is built for students who want to use the law as an instrument of public good. This isn't a pre-law track in the traditional sense — it isn't about getting into law school for its own sake. It's about understanding how legal systems shape society and developing the skills to engage with those systems on behalf of people who need it most.

Fellows in this track will develop strong legal writing and argumentation skills, learn how public interest law differs from private practice, and build an understanding of the federal prosecutorial and civil rights landscape. Programming emphasizes analytical writing, case analysis, and policy-adjacent legal work.

This track is the right fit for students interested in public defense, civil rights litigation, federal prosecution, policy advocacy, or positions at institutions like the ACLU, DOJ, or public defender offices.

Example Careers
  • Public defender
  • Civil rights attorney
  • Federal prosecutor (DOJ, U.S. Attorney)
  • ACLU staff attorney
  • Legislative counsel
  • Policy litigation analyst
Relevant Fellowships
  • Truman Scholarship
  • Equal Justice Works Fellowship
  • DOJ Honors Program
  • ACLU Legal Fellowship
Skills Developed
Legal Writing Case Analysis Oral Advocacy Policy Memo Research

Track Two

Government & Policy

The Government & Policy track is for students drawn to the mechanics of how government actually works — how policy is made, who makes it, and how to influence it from inside the institution. This track covers the full spectrum of federal government engagement, from Congressional staffing to federal agencies to nonpartisan oversight bodies like the GAO.

Fellows in this track will develop strong policy analysis and writing skills, learn how to navigate federal hiring pipelines, and build an understanding of how the legislative and executive branches operate in practice — not just in theory. Programming emphasizes memo writing, policy analysis, and the professional norms of government work.

This is the right track for students interested in Congressional offices, federal agencies (State, Defense, HHS, etc.), think tanks, the GAO, or other nonpartisan accountability institutions.

Example Careers
  • Congressional staffer
  • GAO Management & Program Analyst
  • Federal agency analyst
  • Think tank researcher
  • Legislative affairs specialist
  • Budget/policy analyst (OMB, CBO)
Relevant Fellowships
  • Truman Scholarship
  • Presidential Management Fellowship
  • Congressional Research Service Fellowship
  • Udall Scholarship
Skills Developed
Policy Memo Briefing Documents Data Analysis Legislative Research Federal Hiring

Track Three

International Affairs

The International Affairs track is for students drawn to the world beyond U.S. borders — diplomacy, national security, international development, and the institutions that manage relationships between nations. This track takes seriously the complexity of international work: the language requirements, the regional expertise, the long career pipelines, and the competitive fellowships that serve as the primary entry points.

Fellows in this track will develop foreign policy analysis skills, learn how the State Department and national security apparatus are structured, and build familiarity with the Rangel, Pickering, and Foreign Service Officer pathways. Programming emphasizes area knowledge, analytical writing, and the specific demands of a career in diplomacy or international development.

This track is the right fit for students interested in the Foreign Service, State Department, national security positions, international NGOs, or graduate programs in international affairs and security studies.

Example Careers
  • Foreign Service Officer (State Dept.)
  • National security analyst
  • International development officer
  • International NGO program manager
  • Defense Intelligence analyst
  • USAID program officer
Relevant Fellowships
  • Charles B. Rangel Fellowship
  • Thomas R. Pickering Fellowship
  • Boren Scholarship
  • Fulbright Program
Skills Developed
Foreign Policy Analysis Area Studies Diplomatic Writing Language Proficiency Security Studies
