Fellowship Structure

A Two-Year Journey

The Opus Society is not a club you join and attend occasionally. It is a structured, two-year fellowship with a defined curriculum, assigned mentorship, and real deliverables. Every semester has a purpose. Every year builds on the last.

Year One — Foundation Year Two — Accelerator Mentorship Expectations
Year One

Foundation Track

Year One is about building the professional floor. Most first- and second-year students interested in public service don't know what they don't know — what opportunities exist, how to apply for them, or how to present themselves competitively. Year One closes those gaps systematically, semester by semester.

Fall Semester

Orientation & Foundations

Getting fellows oriented to the public service landscape and beginning professional development from the ground up.

  • Fellowship orientation and cohort introduction
  • Public service career landscape overview — what the paths actually look like
  • Introduction to federal internship pipelines: GAO, Congressional offices, federal agencies
  • Resume workshop tailored for government and policy applications
  • LinkedIn optimization for public service careers
  • Mentor matching and first mentorship meetings
Fall Deliverable

A polished, government-ready resume reviewed and approved by your assigned mentor before the semester ends.

Spring Semester

Communication & Pipeline

Building the professional communication skills and pipeline awareness needed to pursue real opportunities in Year Two.

  • Professional networking etiquette — email, LinkedIn, informational interviews
  • Introduction to competitive fellowships: Truman, Rangel, Pickering, Udall
  • Cover letter and personal statement fundamentals
  • Guest speaker: Clemson alum in public service
  • Career track exploration — identifying Year Two specialization
  • Continued mentorship meetings (minimum twice per month)
Spring Deliverable

A drafted personal statement for a federal internship or fellowship program of your choosing, reviewed by your mentor.

Year Two

Accelerator Track

Year Two is about outcomes. Fellows enter with a professional foundation and spend the year building a career niche, pursuing real opportunities, and producing work that can go directly into applications and portfolios. By the end of Year Two, every fellow should have submitted at least one competitive application and built a genuine professional network.

Fall Semester

Track Specialization

Fellows declare their career track and programming is tailored accordingly. Depth replaces breadth.

  • Career track declaration and track-specific mentor reassignment if needed
  • Track-specific programming: legal writing, policy analysis, or foreign policy briefings
  • Competitive fellowship application workshop (Truman, Rangel, Pickering, Udall)
  • One-on-one application review sessions with Executive Board
  • Alumni speaker series — Clemson graduates in public service
  • Washington, D.C. or state capital exposure trip (subject to funding)
Fall Deliverable

A submitted application to at least one competitive internship, fellowship, or government program relevant to your career track.

Spring Semester

Capstone & Transition

Fellows complete their capstone, contribute to cohort recruitment, and transition into alumni status.

  • Policy memo and briefing document writing workshops
  • Capstone development with mentor guidance
  • Capstone presentations to Executive Board and faculty advisor
  • Participation in incoming cohort recruitment and selection
  • Alumni network introduction and transition planning
  • Final mentorship reflection and career roadmap session
Spring Capstone

A polished policy brief, fellowship application portfolio, or professional writing sample — a real deliverable you can use in applications and interviews.

One-on-One Support

Mentorship Model

Every Opus Fellow is assigned a one-on-one mentor from the Executive Board. This isn't a passive relationship — mentors are expected to meet with their fellows at minimum twice per month and take active ownership over their professional development.

Matching Process

Fellows are matched with mentors based on career track interest, communication style, and goals. Matches are made by the Director of Recruitment & Mentorship at the start of each year and can be adjusted if needed.

Meeting Cadence

Mentors and fellows meet a minimum of twice per month throughout the academic year. Meetings can be in person or virtual. Fellows in good standing are expected to come prepared with updates, questions, and goals.

What Mentors Provide

Resume and application reviews, professional networking connections, guidance on career decisions, accountability for deliverables, and honest feedback on professional materials and goals.

Good Standing

What's Expected of Fellows

Opus is a fellowship, not a club. Membership has real expectations. Fellows who do not meet these standards are not considered in good standing, which affects their eligibility for alumni status and capstone recognition.

80%

Meeting Attendance

Fellows must attend at least 80% of required fellowship meetings and events each semester. Absences should be communicated to the Director of Programming in advance.

2×

Monthly Mentorship

Fellows are expected to meet with their assigned mentor a minimum of twice per month and to come to those meetings prepared with updates and questions.

✓

Deliverable Completion

Fellows must complete all assigned deliverables — resumes, personal statements, applications, policy briefs — by their stated deadlines each semester.

3.0

Academic Standing

Fellows must maintain a cumulative GPA of 3.0 or higher throughout their fellowship. This reflects the standard we hold ourselves to — public service demands excellence in all areas.
